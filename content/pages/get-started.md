## You Just Learned Something Real

The integration problem is old. The AI-assisted solution is new. The moment is now.

We've been talking about AI changing software development for years, but most of those conversations stayed abstract — chatbots, image generators, vague promises about productivity. What we've walked through here is something different: a specific, measurable shift in how real engineering work gets done. The translation layer between systems — the work that consumed 40% of developer time and billions of dollars annually — is being restructured.

You've seen the numbers. You've seen the workflow. Now here's the question that matters: what do you do with this?

---

<SceneCard eyebrow="For You" title="If You're a Developer" index={0}>

The barrier to entry is low. Start today.

**Try it on your next integration ticket.** Open an AI coding assistant alongside your IDE. Describe the integration problem in plain English before you write a single line of code. See what it proposes. Review it critically. You'll learn something even if you don't use the output.

**Read the documentation on one major AI-native platform.** Several platforms now offer AI-assisted integration as a core feature — not a bolt-on. Understand what they claim, and more importantly, understand their limitations. The developers who thrive will be the ones who know when to trust the AI and when to override it.

**Build a proof of concept.** Find two internal systems that don't talk to each other. Describe the integration in plain English to an AI assistant. See how far it gets. The experiment itself is the lesson.

</SceneCard>

<SceneCard eyebrow="For You" title="If You're a Decision-Maker" index={1}>

Before you approve a budget for an integration project, ask your engineering team three questions.

**"Have we tried describing this integration to an AI first?"** If the answer is no, that's the first experiment. It costs nothing and might save three months.

**"What percentage of our current integration maintenance is automated?"** This number tells you how much technical debt you're carrying. AI-assisted tooling can help retire it — but only if you measure it first.

**"What would we build if integration projects took six weeks instead of six months?"** That's the real question. The constraint you've been working around may not be a constraint anymore.

A reasonable pilot: pick one integration project that's been stalled for budget or resource reasons. Run it with an AI-assisted approach. Measure the time, the quality, the developer experience. Let the data inform the next decision.

</SceneCard>

<SceneCard eyebrow="For You" title="If You're a Student or Learner" index={2}>

The best time to understand this shift is before you're inside it.

**Start with the fundamentals.** Understand REST APIs — how they work, what they return, how authentication works. This foundation doesn't become obsolete; it becomes the lens through which you understand what the AI is actually doing.

**Understand data transformation.** Learn to read a JSON schema. Learn to write a field mapping by hand. Once you've done it manually, you'll understand why AI assistance matters — and you'll be able to review AI output critically.

**Understand how LLMs work.** Not at the research level — at the practical level. What does it mean for a model to "understand" an API? What are hallucinations and why do they happen? What is a context window and why does it matter for long documentation?

**Then combine them.** Take a public API. Take another public API. Describe an integration between them to an AI assistant. Walk through the output. Ask why. Ask what you'd change. That exercise, repeated, is the education.

</SceneCard>

## The Invitation

This isn't about replacing engineers.

It's about multiplying what one engineer can do.

The developers who move into this shift thoughtfully — who learn to direct AI effectively, review its output rigorously, and maintain the judgment that machines can't replicate — will find that the ceiling on what a small team can accomplish has been raised significantly. The work doesn't disappear. It changes shape.

The question isn't whether AI-assisted integration will change your work. It already is. The question is whether you'll be the one driving it.
